## Talking points by chapter

### 01:00 — The Lada and the self-driving car
- The story from the post, told live. Eastern European winter, the gear stick comes out in your hand, three brutal weeks, gives up on driving entirely, *wishes self-driving cars into existence*.
- The pattern: technical capability arrives first. Legal, financial, regulatory infrastructure arrives years later. Insurance for self-driving cars is *still* a mess in 2026.
- Translation: human-agent symbiosis already works technically. The infrastructure to actually live and work with it does not exist yet.

### 06:00 — Why my API is more interesting than my resume
- Resume = what you did in the past, frozen, hand-curated, optimized for keyword matchers.
- API = what your professional system can do, queryable, live, auditable.
- The vision: walk into a new company, your agents handshake with theirs in the lobby, you spend the interview talking culture and strategy instead of demonstrating skills you proved 90 minutes ago in their actual stack.
- Why this isn't sci-fi: every component already exists. Personal RAG over your work history. Verifiable AI compliance audits (EU AI Act starting 2026). Cross-org agent protocols (MCP, etc). Just not assembled into a job-search loop yet.

### 12:00 — What "your AI team" actually is, today
- Vlad's stack as a worked example, named:
  - Claude Code (Opus) as the senior builder.
  - Perplexity Computer as the research arm.
  - Gemini for review and second-opinion.
  - Ollama running local models for the queries he doesn't want leaving his Mac.
- The personal knowledge base that feeds them all — what's in it, how it's structured, what makes it queryable.
- The *delta* between a fresh Vlad with no team and a Vlad with this team: not 30%, not 200%. Different kind of work entirely.

### 17:00 — Milena's pushback
- "OK, but who hires *the team*? The company is hiring you. Are they hiring your agents too? At what cost? With what oversight?"
- Vlad doesn't have a clean answer. Good. The episode is honest about that.
- The behavioral question: when capacity becomes a package, what happens to people whose package is smaller? Is this the next layer of inequality, or does it democratize?

### 22:00 — The legal-infrastructure gap
- Specifically, what doesn't exist yet:
  - Employment contracts that name the AI team as a participant.
  - IP rules for what an embedded agent "learned" while you were at a company.
  - Non-competes that scope to humans + their agents, not just humans.
  - Termination protocols that audit what the agent takes with it.
- The unanswered question: if you leave a company after a year, what *can* you take back from the work you and your AI did together?

### 29:00 — Microsoft Work Trend Index 2026
- Cite directly: the working layer is mostly solved, mode-fit is the new professional skill.
- What "mode-fit" means in practice: knowing when to ask the AI, when to delegate to it, when to collaborate with it, when to ignore it entirely. This is the new craft.
- The ownership layer — Microsoft doesn't have an answer either. Nobody does. That's the gap this podcast is going to keep poking at.

### 33:00 — Who owns what we built together?
- Milena leads here — this is the question that maps onto her behavioral work.
- The institutional knowledge problem: when the thing that learned wasn't a human but an agent, who has the institutional memory after the human leaves?
- Suggested framing: maybe the audit *is* the new artifact. The relationship between human and agent becomes a documentable thing, and when the human leaves, the relationship gets versioned, branched, archived.

### 38:00 — Identity is a package
- Milena re-grounds in the post:
> The competitive unit becomes the human plus their AI team. Employer-employee bargains change because the capacity a professional brings is now a package, not just a skillset.
- Social identity theory: people internalize "I am the one who figures things out." That role is now partly delegated. Quietly, without anyone naming it.
- What this means for self-perception. The conversation gets honest here — Vlad on what his identity-as-builder felt like before vs after his AI team became real.

### 42:00 — What to do this quarter
Three concrete practices, named, useful for the listener regardless of their seniority:
1. **Inventory your team.** List the AI systems you actually work with weekly. Most professionals can't, and that's the first problem.
2. **Audit one workflow end-to-end.** Pick one repeating task. Document what you contribute, what the AI contributes, what the joint output is. This is the seed of the "API" version of you.
3. **Have one conversation with your manager about it.** Not about "AI policy." About what you, plus your agents, are delivering. Most professionals haven't framed it that way to anyone. Most managers haven't asked.

### 46:00 — Close
> **Vlad:** Johnny Mnemonic carried data in his head that corporations wanted access to. We've been imagining this for decades, in different registers — horror, liberation, a question we couldn't quite answer. The question is getting simpler now. Do you have an API? Are we ready to work like that?
