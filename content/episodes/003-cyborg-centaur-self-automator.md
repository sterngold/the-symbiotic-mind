# Episode 3 — Cyborg, Centaur, or Self-Automator

The first two episodes plant the thesis. Episode 3 lands the most cited finding of 2026 work-AI research and makes it personal. This is the episode that should hit the Apple New & Noteworthy threshold, and the one that should turn casual listeners into subscribers.

Format change: this is a *joint* episode, not lead/foil. Both hosts run a co-diagnosis on each other and then on the listener. The conceit is real: by the end of the episode, the listener should be able to name which mode they were in this week, and have one concrete move for next week.

## Talking points by chapter

### 01:00 — What the study found
- HBS Working Paper 26-036, Randazzo, Lifshitz, Kellogg, Dell'Acqua, Mollick, Candelon, Lakhani (2026). 244 BCG consultants. Real client tasks. AI tools available.
- Three patterns emerged. Not on a single axis — these are different *strategies for being in the loop*.
- The headline finding: cyborgs and centaurs both outperformed peers. Self-automators *underperformed*, despite using the same tools.

### 06:00 — The three modes, defined
Spend the time. Be specific. Don't paraphrase.

- **Cyborgs**: AI woven into the workflow, judgment intact. The human and the AI think *together* — the AI isn't a tool, it's an extension of the cognitive loop. Speed up *and* quality up.
- **Centaurs**: Clear division of labor. Strategy and judgment human; execution AI. Like the chess players — human captains the AI's moves. Speed up; quality preserved.
- **Self-automators**: Outsourced the thinking too. AI does the work, the human becomes a transit point for output they didn't really evaluate. Speed up; quality *down* on tasks requiring reasoning.

### 14:00 — Why all three look identical from the outside
- Same tool. Same Slack channel. Same time-to-output. Same prose quality on the surface — AI prose is uniformly competent at sentence level.
- The difference is invisible until someone asks: *what came back, and what did you do with it?*
- The reason this matters for managers, hiring, and education: you can't see who's a cyborg and who's a self-automator from the artifact alone. You can only see it from the process.
- Vlad: practitioner version. The two engineers shipping the same PR — one used AI as a thinking partner, one accepted the first answer. The PR looks identical. The next quarter, they are not.

### 19:00 — Live diagnosis: Vlad on himself
- Pick three real tasks from the past week and walk through them.
- For each: what mode was I in, honestly? Where did I lapse? When did I notice?
- Vlad will catch himself in a self-automator moment somewhere — that's the whole point. The episode has to model the honesty it asks of the listener.
- Concrete framing: not "I always do X." It's "Tuesday at 3pm I was in this mode, here's what tipped me, here's what I'd change."

### 27:00 — Live diagnosis: Milena on herself
- Same exercise. Milena does it from the writing side, not the coding side — the audience hears the same pattern in a different domain.
- Specifically what to look for: where did the AI's framing become her framing without her noticing? That's the centaur-to-self-automator slip.
- The behavioral observation: the slip happens fastest when you're tired, when the stakes feel low, and when the AI's first answer is *good enough*. Three flags worth naming.

### 34:00 — The trap
- The most dangerous mode isn't self-automation. It's *believing you're a cyborg when you're a self-automator*.
- Why this is the worst case: cyborgs improve over time because they actively learn from the loop. Self-automators atrophy because they delegate the learning along with the work. People who self-diagnose as cyborgs and aren't, atrophy *and* don't notice.
- The honesty test: when was the last time you rejected the AI's output and went a different direction? If you can't name a recent example, you're not a cyborg, you're a self-automator wearing cyborg clothes.

### 40:00 — How to tell, this week
A self-diagnostic the listener can run in 5 minutes:

1. Pick three AI-touched outputs from the past 7 days. Real ones. Things you actually shipped or used.
2. For each, write one sentence: *what was my contribution that the AI couldn't have produced alone?* If you can't, that one was self-automation.
3. Count. If 3/3 had a clear human contribution, cyborg. If 1–2, centaur. If 0, self-automator.
4. Run this weekly for a month. The pattern is the diagnosis.

Vlad will say: he runs this himself. Milena will say: she's going to start.

### 46:00 — What changes for hiring, teams, education
- Hiring: the interview can no longer be "show me your output." It has to be "show me your process with AI in the loop." Vlad ties back to Ep 2 — this is the API of you.
- Teams: the manager's new job is detecting modes, not just outputs. Most don't know they have this job yet.
- Education: schools and bootcamps grading the artifact, not the process, are training self-automators by default.

### 51:00 — Close
> **Milena:** Pick one task next week. Just one. Before you reach for the AI, write down what *you* think the answer is. Then ask. Compare. Notice where you were better, where it was better, where it pulled you off course. Reply to the newsletter with what you found. That's the practice. That's the whole practice. Everything else we're going to talk about on this show comes out of that one habit.
